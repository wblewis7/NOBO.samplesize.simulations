# NOBO.samplesize.simulations
R and JAGS code for running simulations to estimate the necessary minimum sample sizes for detecting treatment effects on survival or per-capita productivity (chicks/female) of northern bobwhite (Colinus virginianus). From Lewis, W. B., and J. A. Martin. Estimating necessary sample sizes for detecting treatment effects on annual survival and per-capita productivity of northern bobwhite (Colinus virginianus).
<br/>
We use data simulations to assess the necessary minimum sample sizes required to detect a difference in annual survival or per-capita productivity between experimental groups (control and treatment). Treatment effects could reflect, for instance, differences in landscape covariates between patches, differential harvest rates, or exposure to management actions. We simulated data under two different baseline values of annual survival (0.12 and 0.22) and productivity (2 and 4 chicks produced per female). We alss varied simulations based on sample sizes (40 - 400, in increments of 40)  and the treatment effect size (10%, 25%, 50%, 75%, or 100% higher demogrpahic rate in the treatment compared to control group). For example, a baseline annual survival of 0.22 (control group) and 50% effect size corresponds to an annual survival in the treatment group of 0.33. We simulated data collection a occurring at two study sites and two years.

## SurvivalSimCode_publication.R
R and JAGS code for simulating and analyzing annual survival datasets is provided in SurvivalSimCode_publication.R. We simulated weekly capture histories for birds over a 104 week period (52 weeks/year and 2 years). We simulated birds and being captured and fitted with radiotransmitters during the first week of March and the first week of October. We divided start times of birds evenly between experimental groups (control and treatment), deployment seasons (March and October), and years (first and second). For each bird in each tracking week, we calculated the 26-week seasonal survival based on an intercept, treatment effect, and season. Season was defined as breeding (April - September) and non-breeding (October - March). Parameters specified non-breeding survival being ~ 10 points higher on the percentage scale than breeding survival (e.g., 50% non-breeding and 40% breeding survival). We then convert seasonal survival to weekly survival. We model capture histories for each individual through a two-step process incorporating survival and radio failure. We first model the weekly probability of retaining an active transmitter, i.e., the radiotrasnmitter is still attached to the bird and actively transmitting. We then use a Bernoulli model to model the alive/dead state (z) of each individual in each week, conditional on retaining an active transmitter in the week and being alive in the previous week. We censor capture histories between the deployment date and last tracked date (either date of mortality or last known alive date for birds with radio failure) for each bird. We do not include spatiotemporal effects on survival because these had little effect on results in a preliminary analysis. We assume perfect detection of birds during telemetry as in other studies.

## ProductivitySimCode_publication.R
R and JAGS code for simulating and analyzing per-capita producting (chicks/female) datasets is provided in ProductivitySimCode_publication.R. We simulated per-capita productivity via a zero-inflated Poisson model based on 1) the probability of successfully hatching at least one nest during the breeding season, and 2) the number of hatched chicks per successful nest. We model both process based on treatment effects. We specify parameters for each submodel such that variation in productivity is ~ 90% driven by variation in the probability of successfully hatching at nest. We do not include spatiotemporal effects on productivity because these had little effect on results in a preliminary analysis.
